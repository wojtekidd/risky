## 1. Positioning Statement

**For** BC managers, risk officers, and compliance teams  
**who** manage business continuity programs in organizations without the time, budget, or appetite for enterprise implementation hell,  
**Risky** is the business continuity platform  
**that** turns a fragmented, spreadsheet-driven continuity program into a structured, always-current system anyone in the organization can contribute to —  
**unlike** legacy BCM tools, which require months of setup, dedicated administrators, and consultants before delivering any value.

### Short form (internal use)
> Risky is the business continuity platform that puts business, IT, and risk teams on the same page — quantifying and predicting risk scenarios in financial terms so that continuity planning stops being treated as overhead and starts being treated as the investment that protects the business.

## 3. Core Narrative

### The Before / After

**Before Risky:**
Business continuity is the program nobody wants to own. Plans live in Word documents on someone's hard drive. BIA collection takes weeks of chasing people over email. The moment you finish, the data is already stale. When an auditor arrives, everyone scrambles. When an incident actually hits, nobody can find the plan.

**After Risky:**
Your BIA runs in days, not weeks — because business owners answer plain questions with sliders and visuals, not spreadsheet columns. Your IT team maps dependencies without rebuilding your CMDB. Your executives see exactly where the single points of failure are, without requesting a report. And when an auditor arrives, you open Risky.

### The Single Insight (what the whole brand rests on)
> *Most BCM tools were built to pass audits. Risky was built to get business, IT, and risk teams speaking the same language — and to prove that continuity planning is worth every penny.*

### The Silo Problem (why the market is broken)
Every organization has three groups who need to contribute to business continuity — and three completely different languages:

- **Business owners** think in revenue, operations, and customers — not RTO or MTPD
- **IT teams** think in infrastructure, dependencies, and systems — not process criticality
- **BC / risk managers** think in frameworks, compliance, and recovery metrics — not business impact in euros

Current BCM tools were built by and for the third group. They demand that business and IT learn a new language before they can contribute a single data point. Most never do. The BC manager fills the gaps alone, with stale guesses.

Risky speaks all three languages simultaneously — and its advanced risk modelling turns the combined output into ROI evidence that makes the CFO stop treating continuity as an overhead line item.

## 4. Messaging Pillars

Four pillars. Each addresses a documented pain point. Each maps to a product feature.

---

### Pillar 1 — Speed to Insight
**Core claim:** A complete, accurate BIA in days — not weeks.

**Why it lands:** Manual BIA collection is the #1 time drain in every BC program. BC managers spend weeks chasing business owners who don't speak continuity jargon. Risky's Zero-Jargon Impact Wizard and magic-link delegation eliminate the bottleneck.

**Proof point:** Business owners answer sliders and visual impact tools — no spreadsheets. The platform translates their answers into MTPD, RPO, and MBCO automatically.

**Copy variants:**
- *"Complete a BIA in days, not weeks."*
- *"Stop chasing people over email. Magic links bring the data to you."*
- *"Your BIA shouldn't take longer than the disruption you're planning for."*

---

### Pillar 2 — Organizational Usability
**Core claim:** The whole organization contributes — without accounts, training, or meetings.

**Why it lands:** The #1 reason BC programs collapse is that they depend on one person knowing how the tool works. When that person leaves, the program dies. Risky distributes ownership without distributing complexity.

**Proof point:** Magic links let IT, HR, and business owners contribute data — click, input, close. No onboarding. No accounts. No alignment meetings. Roles and ownership cascade automatically down the architecture tree.

**Copy variants:**
- *"Not just for the BC Manager. Built for everyone who knows something important."*
- *"Your IT team maps what they know. The platform handles the rest."*
- *"When the BC Manager is on holiday, the program still runs."*

---

### Pillar 3 — Executive Clarity
**Core claim:** Hidden single points of failure — visible in 30 seconds, no report required.

**Why it lands:** Executives have no visibility into BC posture until an auditor shows up or an incident occurs. The concentration heatmap turns hidden SPOFs into something any leader can see at a glance.

**Proof point:** The management view's asset grid darkens tiles as shared dependencies accumulate — making concentration risk visual, not numerical. One click reveals which business processes collapse if that asset fails.

**Copy variants:**
- *"See your real exposure. Not last quarter's report."*
- *"The management view that makes SPOFs impossible to miss."*
- *"Risk your board can actually see."*

---

### Pillar 4 — Financial Risk Quantification
**Core claim:** Know exactly what each risk scenario would cost — before it happens.

**Why it lands:** BC programs are killed by one question: *"What's the ROI?"* BC managers can't answer it because their tools produce recovery metrics, not financial projections. CFOs don't sign off on what they can't price. Risky's risk modelling outputs scenario-level financial loss estimates — turning the conversation from "is this worth it?" to "we can't afford not to."

**Proof point:** Risky models disruption scenarios against your specific dependency map and business data — projecting financial impact per scenario (revenue loss, recovery cost, regulatory exposure). The BCM owner gets the evidence to make the internal case. The CFO gets the number that justifies the spend.

**Copy variants:**
- *"Know what a supplier failure would cost you. Before it happens."*
- *"Turn your BIA into a financial case your CFO can't argue with."*
- *"Risk modelling that speaks euros, not recovery time objectives."*
- *"The number that turns BC from overhead into investment."*

---

### Pillar 5 — Audit Confidence
**Core claim:** Audit-ready from day one. Every score explainable. Every plan versioned.

**Why it lands:** Black-box AI scoring is a regulatory liability in DORA, ISO 22301, and HIPAA contexts. Compliance officers and GRC teams need to stand behind the outputs they hand auditors. Risky's transparent logic is built for accountability, not for demos.

**Proof point:** Every risk assessment shows its inputs, assumptions, and human-applied judgements. Every plan update is versioned. When the auditor asks why a process was classified as critical — you have a documented answer.

**Copy variants:**
- *"Audit-ready by design. Not scrambled together the week before."*
- *"Risk scores you can actually explain to a regulator."*
- *"ISO 22301 aligned. DORA-ready. Zero-finding audits start here."*

## 6. Per-Persona Message Map

### BCM Program Owner *(lead persona)*
**Their problem:** Weeks of chasing people, stale data, one-person dependency, audit scrambles — and a constant fight to justify the program's existence internally.
**What they want to hear:** You stop being the bottleneck. And you finally get the financial evidence to make the internal case.
**Primary message:** *"Stop managing continuity in spreadsheets. Risky centralizes your BIAs, plans, and audit trail — and models the financial impact of your top risk scenarios so you can finally answer 'what's the ROI?' with a number."*
**Secondary:** Speed (days not weeks), delegation (magic links), financial scenario modelling, audit trail
**Tone:** Peer-to-peer. Acknowledge the frustration directly. Especially the budget fight — they've lost it before.

---

### Chief Risk Officer
**Their problem:** No live view of operational exposure. AI scores they can't defend. Siloed tools.
**What they want to hear:** Real, explainable enterprise-wide risk visibility.
**Primary message:** *"Get an always-current picture of operational dependencies and concentration risk — with transparent AI scoring built for your next audit."*
**Secondary:** DORA readiness, SPOF visibility, cross-functional data
**Tone:** Strategic, data-credible. Speak to board-level accountability.

---

### CIO / IT Leader
**Their problem:** Yet another tool to integrate. CMDB rebuild requests. Implementation overhead.
**What they want to hear:** Minimal lift. No CMDB migration. Your team maps what they know.
**Primary message:** *"Your IT team maps what they know. Risky builds the dependency picture without rebuilding your asset inventory."*
**Secondary:** No CMDB required, top-down or bottom-up mapping, ownership cascades
**Tone:** Practical and honest. No overselling of integrations that don't exist yet.

---

### Compliance / GRC Officer
**Their problem:** Evidence scrambles before audits. Inconsistent plan versions. Non-explainable AI.
**What they want to hear:** Immutable records. Explainable logic. Pre-built templates.
**Primary message:** *"Every plan versioned. Every risk score traceable. ISO 22301 and DORA templates included. When your auditor arrives, you open Risky — not a file server."*
**Secondary:** Audit trail, transparent scoring, regulatory templates
**Tone:** Precise. Use the right terminology (BIA, RTO, MTPD, DORA, ISO 22301). These buyers are specialists.

---

### CFO
**Their problem:** BC spend is hard to justify — the benefit is invisible until something goes wrong. No tool has ever handed them a number that makes the investment case self-evident.
**What they want to hear:** A financial model, not a risk framework.
**Primary message:** *"Risky models your top disruption scenarios in financial terms — projected revenue loss, recovery cost, regulatory exposure — so the investment case writes itself. Not a consultant's estimate. Your actual data."*
**Secondary:** Scenario-level loss projection, transparent pricing, low TCO, no implementation invoice
**Tone:** Numbers-first, no jargon. Treat them as the financial decision-maker, not a risk student. The output should land like a CFO dashboard, not a BC report.

## 8. Language Guide

### Use
| Term | Why |
|------|-----|
| Disruption / incident / event | Neutral, professional — not alarmist |
| Continuity program | Implies ongoing, living — not a one-time doc |
| Stay running | Outcome-focused, active |
| Always-current | Implies live data, not stale snapshots |
| Whole organization | Inclusive — not just the BC team |
| Explainable | Better than "transparent" — auditors need explanations |
| Productive in hours | Specific, credible claim |

### Avoid
| Term | Why |
|------|-----|
| Catastrophe / disaster | Fear-driven, off-brand |
| Revolutionary / cutting-edge | Overused, vague |
| Seamless / frictionless | Overused SaaS filler |
| Best-in-class / world-class | Unverifiable, dismissed by B2B buyers |
| Leverage | Use "use" |
| AI-powered (as a feature headline) | Overused; buyers are sceptical — show what it does instead |
| Enterprise-grade | Implies complexity, the thing we're fighting against |

## Unresolved Questions

1. **Pricing model** — Transparent pricing is a differentiator, but tiers not yet defined. Messaging should hint at accessibility without specific numbers until pricing is set.
2. **DORA specificity** — How prominently to lead with DORA? Strong hook for EU fintech but narrows audience for global positioning.
3. **"AI" messaging** — Research shows 34% of practitioners dismiss AI in BC. Recommend leading with outcomes (speed, clarity, explainability) not "AI-powered" language — but needs sign-off.
4. **SME vs. mid-market primary target** — Messaging currently spans both. A tighter initial ICP (e.g., 50–500 employees, regulated industry) would sharpen all copy.
